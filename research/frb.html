Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs) Produced by Asteriod-Neutron star Collisions

The origin of fast radio bursts (FRBs) is still a mystery. One model proposed to interpret the repeating object, FRB 121102, is that the radio emission is generated from asteroids colliding with a highly magnetized neutron star (NS). The schematic illustration (shown below), from Geng and Huang (2015)., shows the process during an asteriod/comet collision with a neutron star. The left panel shows the object being stretched into a thin sheet as it accretes onto the NS. Magnetic compression reduces its thickness to a few millimeters, while its width expands to several kilometers near the NS surface. The right panel illustrates the resulting hot plasma fan, where a red-orange arc represents the region generating coherent curvature radiation.

With N–body simulations, I model a debris disk around a central star with an eccentric orbit intruding NS. As the NS approaches the first periastron passage, most of the comets are scattered away rather than being accreted by the NS.

To match the observed FRB rate, the debris belt would have to be at least three orders of magnitude more dense than the Kuiper belt.

We also consider the rate of collisions on to the central object but find that the density of the debris belt must be at least four orders of magnitude more dense than the Kuiper belt. These discrepancies in the density arise even if (1) one introduces a Kuiper-belt like comet belt rather than an asteroid belt and assume that comet impacts can also make FRBs; (2) the NS moves ∼ 2 orders of magnitude slower than their normal proper-motion velocity due to supernova kicks; and (3) the NS orbit is coplanar to the debris belt, which provides the highest rate of collisions. Therefore, I conclude that if repeating FRBs are produced by comets colliding with an NS, the progenitor system must be in an extremely rare arrangement (i.e. an intruding NS plummet- ing through an extremely dense Kuiper-like comet belt or asteroid belt) to cause the repeating behavior as observed in FRB 121102.
